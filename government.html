Japan's Government

National Authorities:

The Post-war constitution is the oldest unamended constitution in the world. It is a short constitution with only 5,00 words (average constitution has 21,000 words). In it the Emperor is the "symbol of the State and unity of the people”. He also appoints the prime minister in a ceremonial procedure called the Imperial Investiture. The constitution defines a unitary parliamentary system of government with 3 branches. The unitary system governs the state as a single unit in which the central government is ultimately supreme. The 3 branches are

Executive - prime minister, who selects and dismisses cabinet members. The prime minister is the head of the government of Japan, chief executive of the national cabinet and commander-in-chief of the armed forces. Designated by the National Diet, appointed by the Emperor, he must have the confidence of the House of Representatives. He signs laws and cabinet orders. He must be a civilian and be a member of either house of the Diet. The cabinet appoints the Supreme Court Judges.

Legislative - National Diet, made up of 2 chambers is the sole law maker and may impeach judges, approve treaties and budgets.

  • House of Councilors/Sangi-in (Upper House)245 members. Can veto decisions made by the House of Representatives. Must be of Japanese nationality. 30 years old age requirement
  • House of Representatives/Shugi-in (Lower House) 465 members. Considered the more powerful house, it may override by 2/3 vote a veto by Councilors. Each member is elected for 4 years but the house may be dissolved by the prime minister or a passage of a no confidence vote. 25 years old age requirement

Judicial - independence from executive and legislative is guaranteed in the constitution. The judicial branch consists of the Supreme Court and 4 lower courts

  • Supreme Court determines the constitutionality of law, regulation or official act. Responsible for nominating lower court judges, oversees judicial system, and disciplining judges. The court of last resort, has the power of judicial review. A Judge may be removed by referendum.
  • High Court

    hears appeals to judgements from District and Family courts. Criminal appeals heard directly to High court.

  • District Court

    Civil appeals first done here

  • Family Court

    deals with domestic disputes, i.e., divorce and inheritance, and juvenile delinquents

  • Summary Court

    deal with civil cases not exceeding ¥1,400,000 and minor criminal cases.

Japan has 3 types of elections:

  • General elections to the House of Representatives every 4 years
  • Elections to the House of Councilors every 3 years (1/2 the councilors)
  • Local elections for prefecture and municipalities every 4 years

Both house elections are held under a parallel voting system, where each individual votes twice. In one ballot, he votes for a person to represent him. In the other ballot, he votes for a party in a proportionality of seats to be allocated. If that party wins a higher proportion more seats will be given from a party’s list of candidates.

289 representatives are single-seat constituency, directly elected, while 176 are proportional representation, elected by a party-list system from 11 multi-member constituencies. The 11 multi-member constituencies are proportional representation (PR) blocks. Each block consists of one or more prefectures, each prefecture divided, and larger regions further divided into one or more single-member district.

Half of the Councilor members are subject to election every 3 years, 147 are directly elected plural-seat prefectural constituency from 47 prefectures and 98 are elected from a nationwide list by proportional representation.

Local authorities

Japan is divided into 47 administrative divisions, 43 rural prefectures, 2 urban prefectures (Kyoto and Osaka), one “district” (Hokkaido), and one metro district (Tokyo). There are self-governing cities and towns that are independent of larger jurisdictions. Each jurisdiction has an executive (governor in prefectures and mayors for towns and cities) with assembly and committees. Executives and assemblies stand for election every 4 years.
