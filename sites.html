Japan's Sites to See

Anyone who has travelled by rail in Europe will have no trouble navigating the Japanese rail system. A Japan Rail Pass (JR Pass) is a cost-effective way to travel long distances for foreigners. For those already in Japan, prepaid IC cards and cash can be used at vending machines to purchase subway and rail tickets. IC cards can also be used for the bus system, but there are different systems requiring different IC cards. Local buses in Tokyo and Osaka are a secondary method of seeing the city. In less dense cities, local buses are the main means of transport within. The highway buses operate in daytime and overnight, or only daytime, or only overnight. Bridges connect the four main islands but an extensive ferry system connects the others.

At your destination, to experience a uniquely Japanese stay, sleep at a capsule hotel, manga café, Buddhist temple or ryokan/minshuku.

Where to Stay:

Capsule Hotel

Usually found near train stations in large cities. Capsule hotels provide a single bed for a single person in privacy for 3,000 – 4,000 yen per night. Measuring roughly 1.2 m wide X 2 m long X 1 m high, a capsule has a light, alarm clock, tv/radio, and curtain/door for privacy. Shared washrooms, showers, and toilets, they may have restaurants vending machines, lounges, game rooms, or manga libraries. Eating and smoking are not allowed inside the capsules.

Manga cafes/kissa

Found in most cities in Japan. They are open 24 hours and offer showers and free drinks. Hard to find for foreigners, since the outside signs are not written in English, you can purchase an open seat at a computer desk or a private booth. For 1500 – 2500 yen for 5 – 8 hours you can read the library of manga available or sleep.

Shukubo

means sleeping with monks. In Japan, a small number of temples around Nagano, Kyoto, and a few other cities offer shukubo. In these temples, Buddhist monks provide pilgrims and tourist (practicing Buddhist/not) with overnight accommodations to experience the austere life of a monk. Average costs are 6,000 – 10,000 yen per person, cash only. Futon, breakfast and dinner are included, with shared bathroom facilities. Guests are usually invited to 6:00 morning prayers to participate or watch. All meals are vegetarian and no alcohol.

Minshuku

Family-operated, Japanese-style B&Bs where a tourist can experience traditional Japanese lifestyle. Typical rates 5,000 - 10,000 yen per night per person and include home-style dinner and breakfast. Usually small with only a few guests, the toilet and bathroom facilities are usually shared.

Ryokan

can vary in size, cost, and style. Meant to experience traditional Japanese lifestyle and hospitality, rooms are larger for 2- 4 occupants, covered with tatami flooring and futon beds. Guests are supplied a “Yukata”, similar to a kimono but more casual, it’s used as both a bathrobe and loungewear, and can be worn at all times during your stay, to dinner and breakfast. In some resort towns, guests can be seen strolling the city in them. Meals are typically Japanese haute cuisine. An average stay costs 15,000 – 25,000 yen per person per night.

Sites to see in Japan are a matter of taste but these are the sights you must fit into your schedule while there.

Around Tokyo:

Tsukiji Market

Previously the location for the largest fish market in the world (the market has moved to a new location in Toyosu), the outer market is still there with its many shops and restaurants. Along the crowded narrow lanes shops sell knives and food-related goods alongside restaurants with the freshest seafood. Opening at 5:00 am, customers become part of the city eating sushi for breakfast in the bustle.

Asakusa district

Has the atmosphere of old Tokyo. Easily explored on foot, its main attractions are its many shrines, including the Sensoji Temple, and shopping streets. The Sanja Matsuri, Tokyo’s most popular shrine festival, is held there during the third full weekend in May.

Shibuya district

Packed with shopping, dining, and nightclubs around Shibuya Station, it is the center for youth fashion and culture. The heavily decorated neon intersection gets flooded with pedestrians at each light change and is a popular photo spot. Also located within the district is “Love Hotel Hill”, with its concentration of love hotels, the statue of the loyal dog named Hachiko, and Bunkamura (“culture village”), consisting of a concert hall, cinemas, a museum and exhibitions.

Harajuku

The area around Harajuku station is the center of Japan’s extreme teenage culture and fashion. Teenagers dressed in Kawaii, goth, or medieval manga character style show up there. But just like other districts it has its shopping areas. Omotesando is referred to as Japan’s Champs-Elysees with its tree lined broad street and brand name shops.

Kamakura

A coastal city, an hour south of Tokyo, Kamakura is a small city, popular with tourist, and called the Kyoto of eastern Japan. Its sights include the Great Buddha, the Kamakura hiking trails, numerous temples, and sandy beaches.

Kusatsu Onsen

One of Japan’s most famous hot springs resort, Kusatsu Onsen is northwest of Tokyo at 1200 meters above sea level beside Mount Shirane, an active volcano. The onsen encompasses traditional inns and bathing facilities around it. After active travelling, it is one of the most relaxing experiences found.

Mount Fuji

A nearly perfect shaped active volcano, Mt. Fuji has been worshipped as a sacred mountain. There are 4 trails to the summit, open July to mid-September. Usually popular and sometimes crowded, hiking the trails does not require a guide or any experience. How long it takes depends on which station along the mountain you begin. Standing 3776 meters at the summit, the closest station takes 4 – 7 hours to climb and 2 – 4 hours to descend. At an altitude of 1400 meters, the Gotemba Trail takes 7 – 10 hours to climb and 3 – 6 hours to descend.

Around Kyoto:

Kiyomizudera

A UNESCO World Heritage site, Kiyomizudera is a Buddhist temple founded in 780. Made entirely of wood, its imposing size overlooks the forest below it. The Otowa waterfall at its base is divided into 3 streams. Visitors use cups attached to long poles to drink from one. One stream is for longevity, another is success in school, and the last is a fortunate love life. Drinking from all three is considered greedy.

Higashiyama District

One of the cities best preserved districts, it leads up to Kiyomizudera. Its narrow lanes, wood buildings and merchant shops allow guest to experience old Kyoto when it was the capital of Japan.

Kinkakuji (Golden Pavilion)

a Zen temple with its top 2 floors completely covered in gold leaf. Although not possible to enter, guest can view the temple in a parklike setting across a pond.

Gion Matsuri

The festival of the Yasaka Shrine, over the entire month of July, is the most famous festival in Japan. The grand procession of floats occurs July 17 and is spectacular. Floats are up to 25 meters tall, may weigh up to 12 tons, and are carried or pulled on wheels as large as a person. Dating back to 869, the festival has an almost uninterrupted history.

Osaka - Known as a foodie destination:

Dotonburi

Minami/southern downtown Osaka around Namba station is the cities famous entertainment district, Dotonburi. Open 24 hours, the street runs parallel to the Dotonburi Canal, is one of Osaka’s most popular tourist destination. A must for food lovers, it is a popular shopping and entertainment district lit by neon and mechanized signs.

Himeji Castle

About 90 minutes west of Osaka is Himeji Castle. Another UNESCO World Heritage site, it is a national treasure and considered Japan’s most spectacular castle due to its size and beauty.

Sports Fans:

Professional Baseball

Two leagues of 6 teams each, the regular season is from March to October. It is followed by the Climax series (league playoffs) and finally the Nippon Series Championship series between the 2 league champions. Bleacher tickets can be purchased at the gate for around 1000 yen. The organized cheering and fight songs sung by fans are virtually non-stop during the game and make sitting with the home section a memorable experience.

Sumo

A style of Japanese wrestling, it is the national sport. Filled with ritual and custom, the rules are simple, the first wrestler who exits the ring or touches the ground with any part of his body besides his feet loses. With no weight classes, wrestlers are encouraged to get large. Professional sumo has 6 tournaments, lasting 15 days each, every year in January, March, May, July, September, and November. Tickets for tournaments can be purchased at convenience stores or at the stadium with the lowest price Balcony “C” seat tickets costing 3500 yen each.

Scuba and snorkeling

The Ogasawara Islands (Bonin Islands in English) are Japan’s Galapagos Islands. About 1000 kilometers south of Tokyo, the isolated islands can only be reached by weekly 24-hour ferry ride from Tokyo with prices ranging from 25,000 – 75,000 yen per person one-way. At the same latitude as Okinawa roughly, the climate is subtropical, with unique vegetation and wildlife. To protect this, any seeds not native are forbidden, and camping and off-trail hikes are prohibited. Surrounded by picturesque beaches, snorkelers and divers can swim with dolphins or go whale watching while kayaking around the bay.
